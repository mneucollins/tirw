Alice Austen
was a dedicated photographer of remarkable vision and technical skill,
who recorded half a century in over 8,000 photographs,
each one a masterpiece of narrative and visual clarity.

She lived on Staten island and photographed the boats going in and out the Narrows,
the lives of her family and friends,
early automobiles, tickertape parades for returning heroes,
the working people of New York City, Central Park, New York City in transition
and the lives of women who lived together.
For over 30 years, she lived with Gertrude Amelia Tate.
But Alice Austen's photographs of women together are rarely exhibited.

Alice Austen lost all her money in the Depression, and then she lost her house
and her belongings. Finally, she was reduced to living in the poor house.
Her work was discovered in 1951 and exhibited in the Richmondtown museum.
She died less than a year later.
There is an Alice Austen Museum in the home she was forced to sell on Staten Island.
But if there has been a major museum retrospective of her work,
I am not aware of it.

It seems that like many other artists, particularly women,
Alice Austen's work is under house arrest.
