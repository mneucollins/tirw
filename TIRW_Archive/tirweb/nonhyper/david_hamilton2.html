David Hamilton
BACK A Note on Mark Strand

Mark Strand's poems and translations have influenced many a younger writer. One poet wrote me---this was several years ago---that he had just spent several afternoons in the Library of the United States Information Service in Nairobi, Kenya immersed in Strand's Selected Poems. One result was that he wound up at the Writers' Workshop a few years later and now has a small volume out of his own. Strand has a remarkable way of coming through to writers in the making. He is, nevertheless, something of an enigma, a "difficult poet" whom one reads and rereads, seldom fully certain of what one has grasped. Here, then, a clue.

In Strand's most recent volume, The Weather of Words (Knopf 2000) a collection of miscellaneous prose, you will find him leading off with an ABC in which D is for Dante who, he says, has not influenced him. A cheeky thing to say, and not wholly accurate. How could any poet reasonably well read in the traditions of the West not be nudged by Dante, even if negatively, in the opposite direction? And Strand, like the rest of us of a certain age, most likely took a Great Books course or two.

Dante is one of that handful of writers the West has been pleased to call, in our somewhat self-congratulatory way, "universal." Toward the end of Troilus and Criseyde, Chaucer (c. 1385) made out an early list---Homer, Virgil, Ovid, Lucan, and Statius. We would probably substitute Dante and Shakespeare for Chaucer's last two. One thing they have in common, setting Ovid a little aside, is that they arrive at the universal through the extremely local and particular. To say Homer, Virgil, Dante, and Shakespeare is to say Troy, Rome, Florence, and London. Add Joyce and Proust and the pattern holds.

Which is why we can agree that Dante did not influence Strand or, better, shoved him in the opposite direction, for rarely does a particular of time and place enter his picture. Strand's favorite image is the air filling in behind the person who divided space by walking through it, or sunlight streaming into the arc of a perfect dive as the diver vanishes, or a snowflake melting on the arm of a chair, leaving the air whole again---as if repaired.

Rather than watching the ripples widen from a stone tossed in a pond, Strand notes how quickly the surface smooths out, as if it had not been disturbed, as if the being of which it reminds us were but a momentary blemish. Rather than regaling us with pleasant thoughts about the reach of our influence, he insists on "a solemn waking / To brevity, to the lifting and falling away of attention." Local texture is as nothing compared to the immancence of eschatological transition and the great healing emptiness that will close around our small fissure in things. Isn't that a trip?

So next time you pick up a volume of Strand's, look for another instance of his essential image. His speakers are never more than incidentally in Nova Scotia, Utah, Rio, or Iowa City. Think of them as Anyones pacing off their vanishing. The Weather of Words, by the way, has several other chapters equally sly.
