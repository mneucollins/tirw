Jane Unrue
BACK CHILD, BIRD, BOX

A portion that has been obliterated contained the middle section of the figure of a child (there was an inscription in the margins to the right and left of the figure, this inscription having been obliterated), and if we were to reach around, we would discover, on the other side near the base, the figure of the child again, the limbs of which are there, the middle section is there, although the head, we would know by feel, has been obliterated. The small panel next up below the cross-beam on this side contains another figure of the child, head obliterated, and still proceeding upward, we see in the next panel a figure of the child, much smaller than in any of the other panels that we will have encountered, although in perfect proportion, this figure of the child smaller being, as in the previous panel, only partially draped, head again obliterated; whereas reaching around again and sliding down a little, we discover yet another figure of the child, rendered all but naked here (there is an additional figure just above the child's left arm of a bird supporting itself by a claw grasped firmly to a bough ending with a tuft of foliage, its other claw tucked beneath its wing), the ankles of the child we would recognize as having been obliterated. The head is obliterated. The feet, further down, are there, they're in shoes, and as long as we are proceeding in this way, at the upper edge (the top stone, incidentally, was taken long ago) is the largest of all of the panels. The head has flowing hair that extends to the shoulders, inscription filling the margins to the left and right of this topmost figure of the child on this side, naked, rendered perfectly, although in the hand of the child there is a box that, when compared to the claw, if we were to reach around to the tuft out at the end of the bough, is an absolute horror. It looks and feels like little better than a lump. And it is the faultiness of the execution of this box, combined with less skillful lettering and irregularities in the spacing of the inscription in the margins (a final C gets stuffed into the lower half of an A), along with, reaching perhaps just one last time, the figure of the head-again-obliterated child herself, fully draped and not feeling anything like what we have come to expect---she's not much more than ripples against your hand---that forces us to wonder whether someone might have been compelled to hurry, shoes and hands tucked under, her little neck draped over as well; or whether somewhere, maybe in a dream about looking far ahead at sticks laid down in piles along the endless shoreline, it had been arrived at that lettering and spacing in this panel should be presented with no great skill or regularity, and that the figure, on this occasion, be presented almost entirely covered up.
