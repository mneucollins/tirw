David Hamilton
BACK INVISIBLE CITIES---a late review

Professor Emilio Andante approached me at the cafe after glancing at the book that I read. It was, he offered, the very best guide to the intricate city we found ourselves in. "Turn to page eighty-five," he instructed. "The first paragraph. Have you ever read a better description? And from Kublai Khan, who never visited."
    The Khan didn't visit, he conquered, I reminded.
    "But not Venice. Avoiding warriors on horses is what drove folks out to these islands in the first place, either that or the dream of constructing the city least likely, a marble snowflake on water. Now twelve million of us storm their work each year. But the Khan learned to see it from afar, under the influence of his chatty friend---who in his own book, by the way, described just about the same number of all-but-invisible and improbably named cities as Calvino has him do here."
    My new friend proved to be a professore andante, always briefly at rest in a new position, and so he had taken the name. He pursued his itinerant calling with an almost dogged flourish. His black linen suit had weathered long summers on three continents. His bright sea of a blue silk tie echoed Mediterranean tiles with their hints of almonds and oranges.
    "What are thongs?" Andante asked. Both he and I should have prospered in the twelfth century when late Latin was the lagoon into which Venice, Valencia, Avignon, and Vienna all drained. Neither of us could compose a sentence in it, not with a dependent clause anyway, but such would have been our common project then. We would have rhymed a good many too, whereas now we simply tried to decipher a few plaques on old walls. Our native languages varying, though, I wasn't sure just what he had said---something like "tong," and I let his question pass. Andante seemed to let his attention drift on a canal at our side while he fingered his moustache.
    "Doesn't the Khan get it perfectly?" he mused. "'Princely palaces with marble doorsteps immersed in the water; the bustle of light craft zig-zagging, driven by oars.' I grant you we converse over outboards now, but who put it better? Turn the leaf to Polo's reply. All his cities are versions of Venice, the archetypal city---streets, canals, churches, and squares, but no suburbs---only an interlace of palace, plaza, tenement, and mall. For Polo, every possible city derives from improbable, fading Venice.
    "Now count pages," he commanded, "both forward and back. Calvino places Polo's secret at the exact center of his book. Rather than seeking a mere ending, you learn to wend your way first toward then away from the hub of his wheel. Who's to say that we're not now, at this very table, at the exact center of Venice." While he spoke, I sat counting pages and only found all he said to be true.
    "What's a thong?" Andante repeated. This time I could not pretend not to hear.
    It's that least of garments, that which frames and embraces the posterior, the derriere that smiles so becomingly from the poster in the pharmacy window across the square. Or there, only seemingly shy, under the white skirt you too just noticed passing. Why?
    "I'm a student of minimal difference, especially when it maximizes, and I overheard a verse yesterday that said, I believe, 'They knelt in a throng/ To worship the thong.' That last word threw me."
    Me too, I replied, though that poster's as close as I've ever got. Do you remember more?
    "Something about 'penance.'"
    In Venice I assume. An imperfect rhyme, but what do you expect when Irish cross with the Norse and their progeny roam south. So,

A shining young nun of old Venice
Drove priest after priest to his penance . . .

    "Yes," Andante joined in, "'They knelt in a throng/ To worship the thong . . .' you see, such a sottil, I think you would say 'thin' difference. The slightest omission makes a new, and dare I say rounder, more radiant word."
    Such an absence makes a presence, no? A conference must be convening even now in California. Andante smiled, but not, I think, out of eagerness to attend. He preferred to tell me more about what I read.
    "Calvino's chapters throng too in their way, traversing their own sottil divisions. 'Cities and Memory,' then across the divide, linked and sweetly joined, you'll find Narcoste, how do you say? 'Hidden Cities.' 'Cities and Desire' pairs similarly with 'Continuous Cities,' for what is more continuous than desire?"
    Ah yes, when it ceases to continue, it ceases to be desire. So each set has its partner, drawn together as if murmuring cheek to cheek. "Cities and Signs" pairs then with "Cities and the Sky," for where else have we always looked for signs?
    "Exactly, and what does 'Trade' depend on more than on one's mark or 'Name'? Appropriate for Venice, a city of shopkeepers, don't you think? That leaves the 'Dead' matched with 'Thin,' or as Calvino said, Sottil cities. Most fortunate for Venice."
    Yes, what a clutter it would be if the dead didn't thin out to that cypress-ringed cemetery island across the water that "makes a sottil garland for hir head."
    "What's that?"
    Just a scrap of Chaucer, who may have known Venice too. Dante, Boccaccio, and Petrarch all did.
    "What Oulipo privileges they would have had. Calvino sat with that group, you know, giving as good as he got. Now number the chapters from front to back and again from back to front, 1-55 either way. You'll find the distribution of any set is always an exact mirror image of its partner, 'Cities and Desire,' for example, take positions 3-5-8-12 and 16, and 'Continuous Cities' the same, though counting from the end, from the posterior. One cheek reflects the other you could say."
    Have you ever known an Albert Howard Carter, III? I asked. I think he once charted, elegantly, something of this fugue-like stream.
    "Astonishing, and where did he do that?"
    Well, he began his work at Iowa.
    "Idaho, you say. Never heard of him."
    More's the pity, I thought, overlooking his last less than minimal difference. But fifty-five chapters, that's an uneven eleven sets of five. They can't all be paired.
    "Leaving 'Cities and Eyes' don't you see? Distributed exactly through the middle, with nineteen chapters before its first instance and nineteen after its last, the set strung out on intervals of four, unmatched, and for precisely that reason soliciting our special regard. For where does that thong lead your eye may I ask?"
    To what I do not see but of which I have been known to dream?
    "As to 'invisible cities' --- 'Expressing such in-differance.' I think you've got it now." With an index finger, Andante brushed his moustache as if that motion were his mantra.
    "Campari and soda? Tomorrow perhaps we should stroll the Biennale."
