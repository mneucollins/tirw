Jay David Bolter and Diane Gromala. Windows and Mirrors: Interaction Design, Digital Art, and the Myth of Transparency. Cambridge: The MIT Press, 2003.

Reviewed by Kelly McLaughlin

Windows and Mirrors, the recent book by Jay David Bolter and Diane Gromala, looks specifically at human-computer interaction and interface. This project is framed by a discussion of artworks in the SIGGRAPH 2000 Art Gallery. SIGGRAPH is an annual event, unique in its status as both an academic conference and technological trade show, highlighting the newest technologies and software releases. Digital art has been a component of SIGGRAPH since the early 1980s, but the 2000 conference had the strongest art focus to date.

Bolter, well-known as a theorist and historian of digital technology, is the co-author of Remediation: Understanding New Media (2000). Gromala, while also a scholar, approaches digital technology from the perspective of a practitioner and a curator. As a practitioner, her current work focuses on biomorphic book arts and typefaces, which I'll discuss in more detail later. As a curator, particularly relevant to the book at hand, she designed the Art Gallery for SIGGRAPH 2000. In spite of their extensive and varied backgrounds in digital technology and scholarship, Bolter and Gromala define the audience for this text somewhat narrowly:
"This is a book specifically about digital art, but it is written for digital designers and technologists in general: Web designers, educational technologists, graphic designers working with and in digital forms, interface designers, human-computer interaction (HCI) experts, those who are laying out the future of digital media for business and entertainment, and anyone interested in the cultural implications of the digital revolution" (2).

The particular intervention they perform is arguing that the proper function of "digital artifacts" is to be both a transparent vehicle ["window"] onto information or experience and a reflective experience ["mirror"] -- that is, a potential moment of critical awareness and engagement with the technology itself. Thus they argue that digital art has something to teach more structurally oriented digital designers. Each chapter is laid out as a lesson to this end.

In the first chapter, for example, Bolter and Gromala begin with a discussion of TEXT RAIN, a piece by Camille Utterback and Romy Archituv. TEXT RAIN, as it appeared at SIGGRAPH, took the form of two large screens, placed parallel to one another in order to form a corridor through the center of the Art Gallery. One screen served as a surface for projected video; the other, a backdrop. Colored letters "rained" from the top of the projection screen. Visitors walking through the corridor were then captured on video and projected onto this screen as well. They found that their bodies interrupted the fall of letters, allowing them to construct a dynamically abstract poem through their movement. Bolter and Gromala's discussion of this work focuses specifically on the role played by audience participation:

"TEXT RAIN is not simply an expression of the artist's personality. (That romantic notion doesn't fit digital art well.) Rather, the experience of this piece comes from the interaction of the viewers with the creators' design. TEXT RAIN is as much an expression of its viewers as of its creators; it is what the viewers make of it. Without them, the piece is incomplete, for there is nothing on the screen but the falling letters" (13).

The authors then move from the specific focus on TEXT RAIN to a broader history of the computer as a medium. They use this discussion in order to consider the computer's relation to art and to its larger social context as a movement from functioning primarily as a tool or appliance to functioning fully as a medium. This sets the stage for their discussion of human interaction with computers. "Today, we do not operate computers; rather, we interact with them, and successful digital artifacts are designed to be experienced, not simply used" (22). Bolter and Gromala argue that digital art is integral to the experimentation that pushes digital design and human-computer interaction into new realms of experience. The authors take issue with the notion that good digital design should be transparent. Rather, they argue that digital technologies, when they function best, function as a relation between transparency and reflection. >>
