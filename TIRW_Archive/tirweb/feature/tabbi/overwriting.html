Overwriting
By Joseph Tabbi
with images by Gert Wiedmaier

Mary, the first technician I get on the phone, browses the Web as I, clearing papers from my desk, wait for the drivers to re-install.

One dot... Two dot...

I am rebuilding my system. Really, I am. The data is still on disks, not yet sorted into folders, file structures, and commercial interfaces, not yet touched by the mild boredom of order. I cannot access past emails without first extracting them; messages and attachments stored at different times on various platforms won't easily be recombined; drafts won't easily be merged into a single working document.

The Unknown, a four-man online fiction collaborative, learned from logs and fan mail that the majority of their work is read by people at work.

It was out of boredom, mostly, that I had pressed start on the still new machine only to find that it would not boot. And with boredom, after midnight, I dialed the 1-800 number and reached India at mid-day (guessing by Mary's accent). With boredom I endured menues, entered the que, punched in the service tag number, and accepted trained assurances that 'my problem would be resolved.' First we tried to restart, and then to repair the system. It was only at the final interface, facing the same regretful DOS screen I'd gotten hours before, that I recalled in a rush what had been backed up, and what hadn't. Which essay drafts had been sent out as attachments—and so preserved somewhere—and which held onto and lost. Would I even know which corespondent I'd now be ignoring, which thread would not be taken up, which invitation not answered?

Such a person is addressing you—a collector of daily emails, graphic image archives, and text file attachments more numerous than books. And if a book collection becomes, as it became for Walter Benjamin, an image of the collector—what image of myself, what part of my writing life, is given in those files?

 

 

The story of hypermedia, in which the Web is a recent chapter, begins with a vision of transforming the brain's associative connections into media—media that can be infinitely duplicated and easily shared—creating pathways of thought in a form that will not fade with memory.

Taking up a commission accepted before the system crash, I reset my browser proxy and again allow cookies from impermananceagent.com, co-authored by Noah Wardrip-Fruin. I'd agreed, about a month ago as chance would have it, to be one of the designated browsers for the Agent's presentation at the Whitney Art Portal. My correspondence with Noah, I only just now recall, is among the missing emails.

The Agent tells a personal story, a story of impermanence.

Appropriately, it is a story of lost, garbled, and nearly daily 'Error 404' messages, the fallout from cold war fantasies of world-spanning, intimately connected networks built to last. Think of the DARPA project, famously decentralized and distributed among multiple servers like so many movable weapons silos. The most profound enchantment for the systems-builder is the dream of world extension without loss. Ted Nelson's literary machines, Vannevar Bush's memex are not so far, really, from the vision of rocket scientist Wernher von Braun, who thought that because 'nature does not know extinction, only transformation,' a small step in logic would prove the immortality of the human soul.

So much for the exploratory side of the systems-builder—his eternal image, I might call it.

Would it not be presumptuous of me if in order to appear convincingly objective and down-to-earth, I enumerated for you the main sections or prize pieces of a library, if I presented you with their history or even their usefulnesss to a writer?

Said Walter Benjamin ("Unpacking My Library").

 

 

Consciousness cannot shake the boredom implicit in media systems; by design, consciousness is what is kept out of systems.

We're meant to experience The Impermanence Agent the way Benjamin thought architecture should be experienced: in a state of distraction. Up in the corner of my screen, a small browser window features a scrolling text. There are characters—a Joan, an Anne, a Claudia; boxes of letters sent years ago to a mother, a father, a grandmother. Domestic names, workshop realism. And Zen inspired meditations on materiality and media history.(After the Agent, Noah would go on with Nick Montfort to edit a New Media Reader.) Images fade in and out from scanned photographs in sepia—a technique I've seen before in Web writing. Childhood recollection and cultural preservation are the two poles, in this dynamic image/narrative, representing the private and the public.

The Agent ushers the public Web environment into our personal computers.

As I browse the Web, I load pages into the Agent to be inserted, later, in the scrolling text; fragments of my own reading merge with the Agent's waxing and fading images.

A kind of metafiction, then? bringing text elements autotelically into a self-contained composition? constructing a world?

(Only, it's a corporate agency rather than a human agent that's doing the construction.)

The Agent is a storyteller, and a participant in the story being told. Hence, a self-reflexive fiction?

(Except that details are selected not from the world but from the programmed environment my computer provides.)

The slowly scrolling two-column text, almost readable at once, becomes (in the expanded version presented at the Whitney) seven columns spread out on the page, in four rows. Think of Nam-June Paik's bank of tv's showing the same program on each monitor, but at different times. Like Paik's arrangement, the Agent's layout for the Whitney dramatizes a totality that's there, objectively, but not capable of being grasped, cognitively. Nobody can read all these columns and rows, but the visual effect is awesome.

 

 

Before the crash, I thought I would approach the Agent through names and citations offered in the text. There is Bush, proposing a

future device for individual use... a sort of mechanized private file and library;

and there is Nelson, encouraging the construction of new and more reliable forms of digital storage because

thoughts and minds do not last.

In 1963, when Nelson defined the terms, hypertext and hypermedia had little to do with linking as such; connectivity was not about the maximization of research efforts, and the Internet was not meant to be a global archive of everything, ever. Nelson, it is true, imagined a medium to end all media, where everything said becomes a kind of footnote to everything else. But he also, more interestingly, proposed that a text or media object, once established in a database, could be cited in its entirety—taken wholly, collaboratively, and freshly, into a new composition. Drafts on the way to a final edition could be preserved.

Thankfully, although my most recent notes on Nelson are gone, something I'd written on him was posted on the Web a few months ago. It was part of an Ars Electronica Anthology of Art curated by Joachim Gertz. In chain mail fashion, six Web artists and six conceptual writers would chose their twelve successors, who in turn were asked to chose the next set of twelve, and so on. All would address the question:

In the context of contemporary art, what is your vision of a yet unknown art?

My answer: an art that takes responsibility for what it selects, that cites works whole, not piecemeal...

When I load the Anthology essay into the Agent, my words show up, machinically selected, behind the handwritten scrawl of a child's letter to Nana.

 

 

39 million miles of fiber optic cable now criss-cross the United States, enough to encircle the globe 1566 times.

A result of unregulated markets without any concerted oversight of the six U.S. telecom companies who built the networks.

Today network utilization is a disastrous 2.5 to 3 percent overland. 12 percent under sea.

Sometimes these six companies posed as competitors. Often, however, they would "share" clients and infrastructure, and in a pinch one company could claim another's profits as one's own (for that's what they assumed others were doing with profits they'd reported).

Unregulated in their creation, interchangeable with respect to ownership, accounting, structures of management—networks are nonethelsess fraught with hierarchies. Access is limited to specific directories; interfaces are designed with specific commercial browsers in mind. Modifying shared files is often impossible—even when copying is allowed.

Open sourcing is made to seem esoteric.

(Although not yet in India where Linux, even now, is in serious competition for market share.)

 

Whoever works with a computer on a daily basis, at least for a few years, will soon realise that his own computer resemble more and more to its owner. You share everything with your computer: your time (often even for 13 hours a day), your space (desktop), your culture (bookmarks), your personal relationships (e- mails), your memories (photos archives), your ideas, your projects etc. To sum up, a computer, with the passing of time, ends up looking like its owner's brain.

Say the Web artists known as 0100101110101101.org, whose "Life Sharing" project, at the Walker Gallery 9, is essentially a project of file sharing. "Closer to your brain" than diaries, notebooks, abstract paintings and so forth, my own computer holds more shareable life than traditional media.

Assuming the materials, organized and accessible, exist somewhere in a form that I can go back to.

And those sharing my life files? will they know where to look? what software to use? what platform is best for reverse engineering a life narrative?

 

 

Full hypertextuality today is found mostly in new media arts, not in commercial transactions, not in fiction and poetry. In Trace Reddell's Lit-Mixer, for example, which appeared as part of the MusicSoundNoise thread in ebr. Or a conceptual piece called biennale.py, created by 0100101110101101.org and EpidemiC for the Venice Biennale. These works are not about media. They enact media in their coding and functionality, while at the same time embracing art with their purposeless purposefulness. With his software grove box, Reddell lets readers mix echoing sound clips with quotations from philosophers, literary, and cultural theorists. The Lit-Mixer comes with a manual. It advises users to

          Play it loud, and play it for someone.

biennale.py is a working computer language, Python. It claims to be a benign virus, and its authors—affirming art's irresponsibility—disclaim any responsibility for

the execution of the sequence of instructions belonging to biennale.py has been created for artistic and educational research purposes only. Neither the biennale.py authors, nor anyone else affiliated in any way, will accept whatever responsibility for your actions.

Elsewhere, 0100101110101101.org has proposed an entry in an imagined global venture: the EU-sponsored competition against the actual American Global Positioning System (GPS):

The non-military system "Galileo" is to consist of 30 satellites and cover the entire globe. The EU argues that this decision is aimed at making it independent of the GPS—which is still used for military purposes—by giving it its own surveillance complex.

Instead of representation, mimicry; consumption instead of creativity; parody rather than principled questioning.

Instead of politics, the aesthetics of repetition.

Say the project's critics—in one of many responses bundled conveniently with the work itself.

 

 

In Texas, this morning (02-12-03), GPS devices that can be purchased at Walgreens enable recovery workers to recreate the trajectory to earth's surface taken by each shuttle fragment, back to its breakup at the vehicle's point of re-entry. So much for the system builder's backward-looking reality—what the Germans would call Nachträglicheit, the builder's foreknowledge that the story of what happens only happens after.

The shuttle astronauts were publically declared heroes within hours of the failed re-entry. What had they done? what had they to do with space systems perfectly capable of operating, collecting, and transmitting data without passengers?

Among the 12,000 or more retrieved pieces, computer chips are expected to be intact, recoverable not as objects to be mourned or quarantined and eventually displayed, but as data.

not only a backward retrieval of a vanished event, but also a posting forward, at the remembered instant, to all other future moments of corresponding circumstance.

Writes Richard Powers, reflecting on a classic of naturalist photography.

Assemble the materials, and organization comes for free.

 

 

In the icon leading to MusicSoundNoise (and eventually to Reddell), the multicolored Microsoft butterfly, shown bumping against a data screen, deconstructs into color components, chintzy harmony becoming sound, light, and noise. To enter 0100101110101101.org requires four clicks on a forged Microsoft error message, with the black on yellow exclamation point. Long enough to get you worried that the irritation will never go away, but then five further clicks reveal an incremental message:

Now

You're

In

My

Computer

The Impermanence Agent authors enjoy spoofing on the Microsoft interface, along with Reddell, 0100101110101101.org, EpidemiC, and the MSN editors. So do automated spam programs, whose pop-up Message Alerts I haven't yet figured out how to block.

Web artists do not want better interfaces. Their work is not about (in the Agent's words) creating 'a pleasing interface to a world of information that does not please us.' Rather the work puts existing interfaces to uses for which they were not intended.

And I'm to believe that in electronic environments this can be benign? that the EU project might embrace, if only in imagination, autonomous satellite networks independent of the military?

To believe that the EU and its sponsored artists are all about peacekeeping, but ready nonetheless to rise in opposition to the U.S. military?

 

 

A theme emerges, in these media fictions (more accurately, they are real inventions of fictitious media). Something akin to what the French post-structuralists were pleased to term bricolage, or detournment.

But the poststructuralists were thinking mainly about texts not rockets, citations not samples, the civilizing of savages not the salvaging of authenticity:

The remixer isn't concerned with salvaging authenticity, but with creating a new authenticity.

Writes Reddell, citing Ulf Poschardt in D J Culture.

The one duty we owe to history is to rewrite it.

Said Oscar Wilde.

Filled with joy and pride, we come to believe we have created what we have only heard.

Writes Longinus.

No longer content to cite, overgo, derail, or detourn aesthetic precursors, the artist of the sublime overwrites code.

 

 

They're still around, those archived files of mine, but they're not yet accessible from the newly formatted setup I've got in front of me.

What Vannevar Bush expected of the memex was not just the preservation of cultural data, but our inheritance, intact, of a data structure, what he called the

          intricate web of trails carried by the cells of the brain.

He's speaking metaphorically, and no doubt the metaphor is misapplied to neural networks that are much more chemical than they are electronic. But we can at least be precise about what is, and what is not, capable of being preserved in organic and electronic networks.

In the actual brain, neural circuitry is needed for the creation of a core consciousness, a proto-self.

This core self, however, is not 'us,' for it is not conscious. It is not capable of forming the narratives that make 'us' what we are: beings in possession of our own life stories, selves who can feel what it's like to feel.

A proper analogy, then, for Bush's network and Nelson's hypertext is not the conscious mind, but this nonconscious proto-self. Incapable of narration, the proto-self is nonetheless a necessary forerunner of a conscious, autobiographical self.

Consciousness emerges, constrained by a collection of brain devices whose main job is the automated management of the organism's life.

Writing need not mimic or resist technology, if it is to emerge in electronic spaces whose main job is the automated management of corporate existence.

Writing may be constrained by codes but it cannot itself be a code.

 

 

          A story of impermanence.

Personal in its particulars. Autobiographical, evidently.

Hypertextual, for a certainty.

But what can autobiography, which is narrative in nature, have to do with Web selections, which are not?

I save and store emails, sent and received, every few hours; Web pages also. After a few days they're forgotten. In their current condition, without headers, dates, and a proper file structure—maybe they're past recollection.

The first person I talked to in India called me 'Sir'; I was 'Joey' to the next technician I got.

Both phone workers have engineering degrees, and so do I. After graduation, my B.S. was new and marketable in my own country, but with university grants and freelance editing I afforded myself a few years of grad school in literature. In postcolonial India, my advisors, recently graduated, are working 10-hour phone shifts, on a floor with over 100 desks, in a four-story building, one of three facilities operated by Dell, New Delhi. Each advisor will receive two or three calls per day from individuals like me, whose only option (short of data recovery at corporate rates) is to wipe the hard drive clean.

Jobs are scarce, although Matthew, technician number 2, says it's not so bad as last year.

When: in the 12 months following July 2000, the 4200 companies listed on the Nasdaq Stock Index reported losses of $148.3 billion.

That's slightly more than the $145.3 billion profits they had reported during the five-year boom from 1995 to 2000.

"What it means is that, with the benefit of hindsight, the late 1990s never happened."

Reports Robert Brenner.

 

 

I would have tried doing the reinstallation myself but there are command strings I don't want to go looking for in a manual. Technical solutions are all coded now, so systems are increasingly closed to a free-ranging intelligence. We've made modules of once open networks. Maybe not in India. But soon in the U.S. and Europe networks will be insulated within the media ecology just as effectively as the brain's modules are insulated from consciousness.

          jtabbi, honey, will you stop snoring?

My list of blocked senders will have to be rebuilt as a redoubt against returning spam and viral attachments. For a time, before the crash, a version of Klez had wormed into my address files, and from me inadvertently it reached the files of friends. I'd long since stopped getting the usual global greetings, asking my 'advice,' soliciting my partnership in business, sending love. Instead, the new, more sophisticated spoofware would send me subject lines that I myself might have written or received. I wonder if the same software technology is used by the Agent, when inserting lines from my own browsing into the scrolling text.

The main goal of our virus is just to survive. And, it can better survive when it doesn't do any harm to the host.

Say the artists collaborating on biennale.py.

Working just to work, is how systems work best. Unconsciously. In ignorance of evil.

Most importantly, neither the life-regulating devices nor their body maps are the generators of consciousness, although their presence is indispensible for the mechanisms that do acheive core consciousness.

Says Antonio Damasio.

 

 

Messages and attachments stored at different times on various platforms.

Consisting of citations, clippings, essay fragments, variations on existing texts.

Yet building toward an argument, nonetheless?

 

 

Resisting the dissertation, Benjamin dreamed of a work that would consist of nothing but quotations from other works. The dream of hypertext is related but different—a direct material transfer of texts into one's own writing space, spread out and visible to the eye if not graspable by serial acts of attention.

When a text however is installed as a part of a computer's program,

          the work has no visible representation at all.

As Geniwate says of biennale.py. A work she cites in contradiction of Lev Manovitch's filmic approach to electronic media.

No visible representation, too many interruptions for narrative pacing, less and less that's shareable, free of interference. Very little that's conducive to a sustained communication between minds.

Nothing can be known, as narrative, from computer codes and circuits.

The virtual as such is inaccessible to the senses. This does not, however, preclude figuring it, in the sense of constructing images of it. The appearance of the virtual is in the twists and folds of formed content, in the movement from one sample to another.

Writes Brian Massumi ("On the Superiority of the Analogue").

Consciousness, too, is virtual in this sense, since it only emerges out of the brain's topology, cells, and distributed networks. Consciousness is materially dependent on the brain but not locatable; and its products—thoughts—are also hard to isolate. Only in the movement from one thought to another can we experience consciousness, can we feel what it is like to have a core self and to reference that self autobiographically.

Maybe we move, while browsing, from one page to another. Interleavings, samples, overwrites, juxtapositions. But none of this comes—none of it can come—to narrative conciousness. Under the threshold of awareness, systems operate in microseconds. At the edges of attention, images come and go only a few of which will be selected, held on the screen for a moment's attention. Fewer still will be saved, stored, made available for future recollection.

Not consciousness as such, but this common ability to structure attention, over time, is the proper analogy between minds and computers.

          To determine what's left out, and what's left in.

Writes Peter Gabriel.

What at any moment can be gone back to, held in mind, worked over, remixed. What is shareable among consciousnesses separated but focused on a single object—a stable text, for example.

The surfacing of code, the consciousness of constraint, makes demands on readers. The media fictions I've looked at call to mind how dependent I am on an entire array of technologies and institutions.

Until the institutions, technologies, codes, and constraints can be forgotten, however, as the conventions supporting print were forgotten with the book's naturalization, narrative's emergence is unlikely in new media environments. Designwriting, codework, the hypertextual embedding of texts within texts still require, of readers, cautious collaborations with systems designed to keep 'us' out. It's the achievement of first-generation Web fictions to show us the extent of that exclusion, the paucity of a life lived with files. These are embodied fictions, to be sure, but such an embodiment ravages consciousness by bringing its material supports to the surface.

When the only sign of embodiment is the wrinkled brow, that's when we'll have Web literature worthy of the name.
