"ART WHILE DOING OTHER THINGS"
 AN INTERVIEW WITH GISELLE BEIGUELMAN

Ingrid Ankerson: We're delighted to feature your work in this first issue of the new IR Web. Do you see your work as an extension of concrete or visual poetry?

Giselle Beiguelman: Yes and No. I really like concrete and visual poetry and I think I was influenced by "concrete poetry ways" of composition.

But I think those genres generally aim to make textual meaning emerge from the visual surface--and Poetrica generally aims at just the opposite. That is, it means to create visual meaning from non-textual characters, exploring new boundaries of non-phonetic language.

Computer-based work introduces algorithmic aesthetics into our cultural and artistic practices, and I'm very attracted to that.

IA: Can you name some concrete or visual poets, contemporary or
historical, that our readers should be or should become familiar with?

GB: I grew up in São Paulo, an important center of visual and
concrete poetry, where Augusto and Haroldo de Campos and Décio Pignatari worked (and still work, although Pignatari is now living elsewhere). They were very important for my generation.

Other favorites include Braga, who explored different ways of writing
(sometimes drawing, sometimes using different materials and surfaces--like stones and wood; Paulo Leminsky (who wrote amazing haiku); and André Vallias.

IA: Giselle, talk a little about the process of writing the algebraic
equations that make up your "no-poems".

GB: I think I am developing in "Poetrica" some issues that I explored in my first cyber-work (The Book after the Book). The epigraph of that work was: "at the intersections of words and symbols we begin to redefine our boundaries..."

"Poetrica" has this statement in its core! Also, in "The Book," I wrote a lot about the novelty of hybrid/digital culture, the mix of phonetic and non-phonetic substrata.

The process of creation on this new project, nevertheless, has been romantic, mysterious. I'm researching many kinds of fonts and "dings" and some of them "force" me to do something. I experiment a lot, superpose them, cut, divide, multiply layers... sometimes I try to do make changes with vectors and suddenly I'm headed on a whole new track. So there is a lot of "Sturm und Drang" in it.

IA: You say this project is also conceived for PDAs. Do you consider
this an early attempt at a new genre of New Media art?

GB: I think PDAs and Mobile phones are the icons of our time. They made us proto-cyborgs, supporting our nomadic way of life. We need those devices because we are always on the move, but to create art for those interfaces is a challenge. The question now is: how to conceive of an art form made to be experiences"in between" --that is, while doing other things?

"Poetrica" also explores the phenomena of the "second-hand original," which is constitutive of digital culture.

All of my "no-poems" are conceived for different interfaces (Web, PDAs, plotters, old printers, etc). They are identical, but their "reading interfaces" are different.

IA: One last question then. Are you influenced in any way by music?

GB: Yes, always music: Piazzola, Vinicius de Moraes, Baden Powell, Cassia Eller, Eurythmics, Paralamas do Sucesso, Lobão, Arnaldo Antunes, The Smiths, Frente, New Order, and Gilberto Gil. I've been listening lately to The Best of 386DX by Alex Shulgin.
