AN INTERVIEW WITH WILLIAM POUNDSTONE
BY BRIAN KIM STEFANS

I first came upon William Poundstone's work when Kenneth Goldsmith posted alink to Poundstone's massive Shockwave site, "New Digital Emblems," on the
Ubuweb listserv.

This piece seemed to go against the grain of much internet text art and poetry at the time in several ways. For one, it involved little HTML, but was entirely contained within the Shockwave file, making it inaccessible to web spiders and text algorithms - that is, it has a "gated community" effect to those of us that like to pillage other web sites for texts for our own works. Further, it was all very carefully "written," not adherent to random or algorithmic processes and avoiding anything suggesting "deconstruction," and the imagery and text all had a cypher-like quality, rather than being oriented toward surface effects or the one-liner ethos of much concrete poetry.

The "New Digital Emblems" site also foregoes the feel of web art sites that celebrate digital technology with beautiful, often mystifying, interfaces, imagery that suggests a career in the graphic arts, and heavy sound elements, often of a light industrial, techno feel.

I love these kinds of sites, but Poundstone's project, despite its being "multimedia," was startling for avoiding any of the trappings of techno-culture, while at the same time suggesting a deeper historical tradition for the "interface" - indeed, suggesting the heroic emblem, with its iconic image, motto, and explanatory text, as being a pre-digital analog.

What is most striking, though, once you reach the main interface--a great spiral bedecked with largish circular icons and emanating from a conch shell in the middle--is the easy dexterity with which Poundstone negotiates several different aesthetic traditions: poetic and imagistic traditions dating back to the Rennaissance, modernist/postmodern photography, collage and literature (Surrealism and Oulipo, most obviously), not to mention the developing traditions of web art itself which he seemed instictively to know how to exploit to create an unmistakably "literary" site that, nonetheless, would not have been done justice in a book.
