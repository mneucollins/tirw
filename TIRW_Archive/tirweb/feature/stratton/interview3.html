One Halloween the family made the trek to find halls filled with twisted orange crepe paper and excited children. "Then the lights went out. There was a power outage. Suddenly, it was a cacophony, screaming and wailing. I took my brother’s hand and we stood still in the dark. When the lights came on, there were kids beating their heads, throwing themselves, against the walls." This first-hand witnessing of life behind the closed doors of Paul’s world began to train her artist’s eye.

She trained in photography at the University of New Mexico, then considered the best photography program in the country. Part of her master’s degree requirement was a written "dissertation" on photography. Stratton chose to look at the history of allegory in photography and studied the work of Robert Frank, who was awarded the first Guggenheim in photography. "I found an article about allegory in "October" magazine, and read every book he footnoted," she said. "I tried to prove that the medium is allegorical —it is always a reinterpretation of sign and symbol.

"Frank would shoot in very low light," and, Stratton argues, "the grain of the photograph itself is a transmitter of the message of the photograph," which got Stratton thinking about other optically oriented "message givers. "Frank’s story is an interesting one," she added. "

Her work, which covers abandoned mental institutions as well as famed sites like Alcatraz and Ellis Island, is essentially based on what abandoned structures call to mind.

"It’s like being a kid and finding a deserted house in the woods —trespassing, afraid, fascinated, dangerous, knowing it once had a life in it that no longer exists.  I think these abandoned places are ‘haunted’ by what has happened in them.  There is some kind of reverberation, like the past itself is an allegory of memory." 

But Stratton is as interested in the technical side of her art as with the individual compositions. "For me, it’s about composing, correcting, figuring out the light, everything has to be perfect. It is demanding, difficult. And so am I." She sipped her wine. "I mean, Jesus, some of the exposures are five minutes long."

When I looked through the images, glimpses of the project she calls "Abandoned Naples," I suddenly felt myself pulled centuries back.  but vivid and exhilarating, of the greatness of place described in a series of isolated images, of the splendour of once-great churches and now devastated catacombs and chapels —Napoli, Ellis Island, Roma —of the whole world as it once was and the world that those places had then created.

Ours is a world now in turmoil; yet the tunnels into soft volcanic rock, the lines of black eye sockets staring out from ancient skulls, as though waiting across the years for me and only me to come and gaze back at them, tell another story. The world is on fire, they whisper. And it has been for a long, long time.
