An Interview with Margaret Stratton, page 2

I met with Margaret Stratton in her Iowa City, Iowa, home on a bitter cold January night. In the bungalow she shares with her partner, Meredith Field, she sat on a 1940s couch salvaged from her parents’ home in Washington State.

I had come in through the back door, by the kitchen, and as I pulled off boots, I looked up at an enormous black and white image of a charred building.

"That’s Wesleyan College, here in Iowa," she said, spotting my gaze. "Some disgruntled student burnt down the music school. I’ve always liked what fires do: the transformation and the inevitable gothic results." She smiled at me.

Her living room is painted dark green, a shade, she tells me, selected after she saw it used in a James Bond film. A string of chili pepper lights ropes around the room. On the television, a new television show premiered: Shari Belafonte and other B-team stars judging "pet stars" brought to the cameras by eager owners. One of Stratton’s two Siamese cats slept atop the TV. "Neither of my cats will be on that show," she said. "One is too smart and the other is too dumb." The smart cat looked at her and she nodded. "I’m right, aren’t I?"

On the floor, her most recent work was spread out. The series, all shot in Italy, began with one trip in 2000 to Napoli, and continued during the summer and autumn of 2002. She quoted Walter Benjamin while flipping through a cream-colored box of over-sized black and white prints. "History is a ruin," and sipped a glass of chardonnay. At 49, she has received five National Endowment for the Arts grants, in both photography and video, among other awards, and has taught at the University of Iowa for 17 years. "I studied in Rome as an undergraduate and when I decided to start shooting in Naples in 2000, I started with trashed, abandoned convents and churches, and that evolved into looking around catacombs. Which are unbelievably weird."

She points to a photo of skulls and femur bones, a sort of human pagoda, close to one-story in height. It looks like something the Khmer Rouge could have created during their decimation of Cambodia. "Around Napoli, the catacombs had been carved from tufa —volcanic —rock. It’s soft and easy to work with; it also makes for a very distinctive, more cathedral-like effect. In those days, the early Christians could gain penance by being bone arrangers." She gazes at the photos, then looks up at the television, where a dog walked on his hind legs. "That is so undignified."

* * *

Stratton talks about how her work is essentially about transformation and the sublime —as well as her quest to make the perfect print. But as she gazes at sheets of crystal-clear black and white images, fine detail radiant from corner to corner, bones looking so real you want to brush volcanic dust from your hands, she sighs. "Who is going to want to buy these images?"

It is fundamental tension for Stratton, being a visual artist with a desire to see her work in collections, on walls, in books. Her images are disturbing at times, and speak to a battle for technical perfection many simply cannot distinguish.

* * *

Born in Washington State to parents who migrated west —her mother from Fargo, North Dakota, her father from Pennsylvania, she sees her art specifically informed by the events of her childhood, specifically the institutionalization of her mentally handicapped brother, Paul, and her mother’s fervent desire to see her strikingly beautiful daughter become Miss America.

She recalls in vivid detail when Paul was sent to Rainier Institute for the Mentally Retarded. "If you see photos of it, it reminds you of a 19th century orphanage," she said. Inside, the huge dormitories were filled with children of all ages, from infants to teenagers. After Paul was placed in the orphanage, the family made monthly excursions to take him to lunch. Part of this routine included stacks of new clothes, each piece with a hand-sewn label: Paul Stratton. Yet when they arrived the following month, Paul’s closet would be empty, all of his fine things offered by staff to other, less fortunate children. >>
