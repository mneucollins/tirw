An Interview with Margaret Stratton by Leslie Roberts

"They represent a golden mean of biological success, which excludes equally the rough and the weak, the boisterously animal and the over-refined. They have no hesitations, nor secret thoughts, but stand up in the open and concentrate whole-heartedly on what they are doing.
                         --Kenneth Clark, Landscape into Art.


Margaret Stratton’s photography is not an invitation to revel in the lush experience of humanity. Instead, Stratton pushes your eyes toward the dark alcoves, the crumbling, stacked skulls, the collapsed ceiling, the angel gathering dust atop a long-forgotten altar.

As Professor of Photography at the University of Iowa, Stratton advises her students to create work that "makes people want to keep looking at it." As an artist, she seeks access to places most of us will never actually see —locked catacombs in Southern Italy, aging, abandoned prison cells littered with rusting bed frames and curling mattresses, the jagged metal ectoskeleton that was once the World Trade Center.—

For some time, her mind was occupied by what remained of the Twin Towers in the brief period after September 11 but before salvage crews leveled the site.

She flew to New York in October, 2001, and hearing repeatedly "no photos" from armed National Guardsmen, soldiers guarding the site, she found access through a parking garage. So she rented a "really long telephoto lens," then took the Battery Park parking garage elevator to a top floor. Surrounded by cars, covered in dust and ash, she notes, "I knew no one was ever going to claim those cars. And as I stood there, the sky behind me glowed with an eerie red hue, literally the color of blood." >>
