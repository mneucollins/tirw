Amy Sara Carroll interview, page 2

HB: Some of the works in this collection seem like performance poems. "Breathe," for example, would probably do well in a Nuyorican Café-style slam, while others suggest a jazz influence. Have any of these works been performed?

AC: It doesn't surprise me that you write this. I study Mexican and U.S. performance (mainly work based in the visual arts) and alternative art in the parallel universe of my academic research and prose. Tangentially, I would claim the 80s/90s U.S. tradition of performance poetry as an influence. And, although I cannot even read music (it's embarrassing to admit to one's illiteracies in an interview), I would say that these poems owe a great debt to various musical traditions, including jazz. I have given several readings -- not translatable into "performances" but I think I feel comfortable claiming I have a distinctly "performative" style of presentation. Moreover, I wrote these poems by reading (sometimes half-singing) them aloud; meaning, I depend upon hearing (as well as seeing) the momentum of the image and the word.

HB: Would you discuss the role of sexual imagery in your work? You use a number of explicit sexual references both in your language and in the visual images of the print-poems, yet I wouldn't say the collection is "about" sex.

AC: No, I wouldn't say the collection is "about" sex. In fact, I might be more inclined to claim the collection to be about negotiating relationships between words like "sex," "love," "desire," "intimacy," "intersubjectivity," between categories like "the political" and "the aesthetic." But, any narrative of intentionality, as we're taught to believe, is fraught, fragile, easily perishable (or, at the very least, sports a remarkably short shelf-life) (so, perhaps what I claim above only demonstrates that I have a habit of generously scarequoting). Take #2: sexual imagery in Secession facilitated my speaking about certain forms of subjectivity, which I didn't think were receiving enough and/or any attention on the greater radar screen(s) of pop & high cultures (I know, I know, I'm making a dubious distinction…).

HB: I assume the lesbian subject position is one of those "certain forms" you're referring to. It's interesting that although the "I" of the poems seems to resist most identity categories, the lesbian subject position seems to remain unchallenged. Why is that?

AC: I think I would say the identity category this collection claims is the "queer subject position" (understood as the mixed, the hybrid) as an impossibly impure vantagepoint (versus the lesbian). It seemed to me absolutely essential strategically (pun intended) to bring sexuality to the table in Secession. I was angry and put out by events in this country like the Senate confirmation hearings of Clarence Thomas (the disavowal of Anita Hill's testimony!) and the Senate hearings on gays in the military. I was (and continue to be) irrevocably shaken by U.S. inattention to AIDS, by the country's refusal to grant full citizenship to (sexual) minority populations, by the ever-increasing gap between the haves and have-nots. This collection offers up some of my reflections on the political climate in which I lived when I wrote it (between 1994 and 2001). If the "lesbian subject position seems to remain unchallenged" in Secession it was because in every other space it was attacked daily and I saw my writing as a kind of sanctuary against besiegement.

But, lest I continue to sound defensive (or perhaps to maintain that very defensive offensive), let's fastforward to some of the present's own states of emergency: I'm struck now by the limitations of what Anna Deavere Smith has called "generating safehouses," even as I harbor the sensation that it's absolutely necessary to keep the gas pilot of the utopic lit. So, in my recent poetic endeavors, I find myself "challenging" this "queer subject position" as well (insider advantage). To get to this point, however, I needed to establish a homebase (in the guise of Secession), to give myself a fighting chance to imagine the fluidity of an unfolding present, where the risk of loving someone or something would be a democratic proposition, as perishable as any myth of sovereignty/narrative of intentionality. Finally, I began Secession with the premise that I intended to write a feminist and queer collection that would explore some of the contemporary default lines of identity -- notably, race, gender, and sexuality. >>
