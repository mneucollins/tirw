IF ONE SUCCEEDS IN SECEDING,
WHERE HAS ONE MANAGED TO GO?
an Interview with Amy Sara Carroll


(click to enlarge "LL")

Secession, Amy Sara Carroll's collection of poems, is divided into three parts: "Secession," which splices together a variety of poetic forms,languages, and images; "The Poem-of-the-Month Club," which experiments with the prose-poem; and "A Good Badgirl's Alphabet," which is modelled after a child's reading primer.

HB: The title of your collection "Secession" says a lot. The word itself signifies a withdrawal from an association, organization, or alliance, and the poems certainly seem to resist not only personal identity classifications based on characteristics such as race and gender but also poetic classifications based on such categories as genre or affiliation with a movement. From what would you say these poems are seceding? Are they making an attempt to get outside of categories, especially oppositional ones, altogether?

AC: I meant the title of this collection to operate on multiple planes. I wanted a reader to hear the resonances of political secession (although not in U.S. terms, which would script some opposition between a North and South, unless perhaps, one wanted to think of reclaiming that geographic configuration to mean "a global North versus a global South"). I also wanted the reader to recall the Viennese Secession movement and that of Photo-Secession, to remember the ways that "secession" as a term has been used in artistic venues. I suppose I would say I created these poems to secede from certain limited worldviews I myself had acquired (from the left, from the right, and from the meddling, middling [neo]liberal), that I wanted to write myself into another space of politicized affect.

Of course, my explanation betrays a hopelessly utopic sentiment, one, which ultimately I found myself questioning/dismantling within the collection (bless that deconstructive impulse!). Put differently by the time I had a draft of Secession in hand, I felt I'd
beached myself on a desert island that smelled faintly of the Enlightenment and/or the bevy of modernisms.... Which only proves: we carry lots of baggage on-board, even when our so-called intentions are to travel light(ly). And, yes, in response to your ultimate question, in this collection I desperately was attempting to live outside of categories, especially oppositional ones, to the extent that in the writing of these poems, I had many imaginary interlocutors, including myself.

HB: So would you say that part of what you discovered is that it is not possible to live outside the categories altogether?

AC: Yes. Or, perhaps I'd say that I "(re)discovered" that it wasn't even desirable to attempt to do so, that absolute affirmation or dismissal of an idea or category at times is more problematic than the category itself, that a process of disidentification (in the way that José Muñoz floats the term) depends upon recycling certain elements of that which seems impossible, that an osmotic relationship exists between the so-called outside and inside worlds. >>
