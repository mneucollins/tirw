Amy Sara Carroll interview, page 4

HB: "To participate in certain rhythms outside of the prescriptive" -- I'm intrigued by your choice of words here. In some of your poems there seems to be an almost Mother Goose-like rhythm, which I certainly associate with certain prescriptive social codes. But in "San Patricio" there's a clear jazz influence in the way the language riffs off of regular rhythms and expressions to bend them into something new yet still connected. Is this kind of rhythmic performativity what you mean?

AC: Well, I will say that "San Patricio" harbors a fairly obvious reference to James Baldwin's short story "Sonny's Blues." I have loved Baldwin's work from the moment I was introduced to it, but that story has particular resonance for me -- both in terms of the subject matter it treats and the way that Baldwin uses language rhythmically to contemplate Sonny's relationship to music. I'll also offer the obvious observation that "San Patricio" attempts to create a Spanglish rhythm insofar as it shuttles between, puns in, and merges Spanish and English.

You are not the first to tell me that my work exhibits a "Mother Goose-like rhythm." In response, I'll repeat that I tried to be quite deliberate in the formal choices I made while writing this collection in the hopes that a symbiosis between Secession's form and content would be apparent to the reader. Many of these poems return to certain "primal scenes" of childhood (for instance, "Watchfulness" or "Tick-Tock") to underscore the temporal fluidity of memory. A Mother Goose-like rhythm recalls the "poetry of childhood," while also lending itself to momentum, revision, and/or accumulation. Literally, words begin to "stick" to each other when rhyme and rhythm intersect. I tried to put some of that stickiness to work for me. Of course, many poets have rewritten "Mother Goose" or childhood narratives (Anne Sexton, for instance, who also uses these narratives and forms to question the construct of femininity). This said, when I wrote "certain rhythms outside of the prescriptive," I again meant in terms of BOTH form and content. Alternately parsed, I am intensely interested in the rhythms of sound, but also the various vibrations of the self (Circadian rhythms included, although that does smack of a certain essentialism).

But, I'd reiterate that I wanted to write a collection of poetry that looked and sounded distinct (i.e., distinct from contemporary U.S. poetry). I underscore "collection" because that was one of the central very simple questions for me: what constitutes a collection (which sounds to the ear as well like, "What constitutes a collective?")? And, concomitantly, what makes poems hold together, what kind of rhythms can emerge over the course of a body of work? How does one establish a recognizable grammar for "ungrammatical bodies," not necessarily normative, but drawing upon certain linguistic tenets? How does one write a single poem, which obviously belongs stylistically to a larger project? I thought, "I want this collection to operate dialectically, for one poem to represent, to perform the whole and vice-versa." And, I realized this manner of thinking was a luxury I'd permitted myself insofar as the expansive conceit of a "collection" gave me ample time and space in which to take hitherto unimaginable liberties with the interconnection between languages (on the most basic level, my attempt to create an alternate primer) and worldviews.

HB: I'd like to ask you about the methods you use for creating these works, especially given your interest in revision and the prescriptive. Are any of the poems procedural? I'm thinking in particular of "D," which is written in concentric circles and in which most of the words begin with the same letter. Obviously words which use the letter "d" are given preference here, but did you use any other compositional rules to create this poem?

AC: Not procedural, per se, although "D" has a wonderful history. I grew up in South Texas (please send money there to bolster resources for public education). My mother is an art teacher at Windsor Park elementary public school in Corpus Christi. Once when I was visiting she asked me to instruct a class -- to show the students my work and teach them how to make a linoleum block print. I was embarking on "D" at the time and I had a brainstorm. I wanted to create a present for these students and for my mother (whose name is Donna). I solicited from them their favorite "D" words at the end of every class period (all the students were fifth-graders, but there were several sections, because art is taught on a rotating basis, i.e., my mother teaches ALL grade levels, and so fifth-graders come on Fridays, one classroom after another). The students thought my desire for words was really funny, just as they loved the title of the sequence "A Good Badgirl's Alphabet," and my streaming narrative of civil disobedience. After I'd gathered all their little sweaty pieces of paper/contributions, I began to write the poem.

I imagined the letter "D" as a stone thrown into the middle of my composition. I then sought to envision the ripple effects of ideas and languages akin to some butterfly theory of chaosmosis and, voi là, "D" emerged as a series of concentric circles. Notably, I included every word I was gifted in the hopes of generating an assemblage (obviously students liberally borrowed from their neighbors to repeat certain words -- isn't recycling marvelous? -- yet, in this context, their borrowing didn't hold a candle to mine). I did provide connectors/approximation of transitions, but some of what appears as such, i.e., "a word i earned for when things go wrong" is actually an explanation a student included with his suggestion (e.g., that notation was meant to teach me the meaning of "dinglehopper," and, predictably, I had the socks charmed off my feet by that gesture of solidarity… I mean, who doesn't need "a word for when things go wrong"?).

So, the poem reads: D/Didgeridoo./Diamondback./Whole-in-one. Ditto. Dingbat. Diminutives of de-/rivatives. Deuteronomy. Disbelief. Systems. Theory. Disaster. Re-/lief. Devanagari. Endangered. Specie. (Dracula. Dingo. Dada.) The devil's blued/her dungarees. Damnation: my devotion: port of intrigue. Two marks dart the neck (dry, clean)./Dash-dash--dinglehopper--a word i earned for when things go wrong. It's always darkest before the dawn./A doughnut, dizzy as a dictionary (lo$$/found): one more dumpling dimpling a town./(Domino affect) (deviation, divine): dinosaurs lumber, damsels distress./The diaper is dirty. Well, den. Change it. >>
