An Interview with David Knoebel on “Heart Pole”
By Rita Raley

RR: How would you characterize your current work in three dimensional text and/or spatial poetry?

DK: I tinker with words in space. I like the cantankerous Rube Goldberg  feeling that Auden caught in his notion of the poem as a "verbal  contraption"; Williams's "machine made of words" seems bland by  comparison, more Ford Fairlane than Stanley Steamer.

I'm just getting back to Click Poetry after completing several  screenplays. I realized that I don't have the temperament to see a  script through production. For one thing, there's just too much  teamwork required. However, the experience taught me how to stick with one project for an extended period of time. When I was first working on Click Poetry, I couldn’t stay with a piece more than a few weeks. Commitment anxiety.

RR: What is the significance of the word or letter’s becoming three-dimensional? Can you comment on what difference it might make for poetry, current paradigms of reading, our understanding of text, etc.?

DK: I don’t know. I used three dimensional letters in my early VRML work and never answered those questions to my own satisfaction. I realized that I was not interested in the thickness of letters, but in the interplay of words and space. So I went to flat letters in three-dimensional space.

RR:  This is interesting because the individual works in Click Poetry are quite dense, semiotically, conceptually, and formally. One gets the sense that each piece required a tremendous commitment in itself.

DK:  Most of them develop in my mind over a long period of time and I keep sketchbooks of ideas. But once I decide to work on something, the work goes fairly fast. I code mostly by hand, and I make (lots of) mistakes. Those, too, may be sources of inspiration. 

RR: What do you find yourself able to achieve that was not possible with previous reading and writing technologies?

DK:  I like to put words in new spatial relationships. Can I surprise  myself by playing with their positions and orientations? By animating  phrases through xyz coordinates? Can I enhance, open up, deepen or  charge the words in ways that aren't possible when they're confined to the page? See also David Smith, The Letter (1950).

RR: Do you find yourself thinking about different reading practices as well? I tend to think that there are conventional modes of signification at place in Click Poetry but also new configurations of text, not only swarming, broken, and/or illegible words but also reading along the z axis. What I find compelling about Click Poetry and some of the cube-based works that will be featured in TIRW is that they do challenge the top-centric and left-centric biases of screen- and page-based reading.

DK:  "Heart Pole" highlights other kinds of reading as the viewer maneuvers the text into readable positions. A readable position may be upside-down and/or backwards; at the top of the screen, at the bottom; in front of, or behind, the other elements
