Editor's Introduction
by Ben Basan

Rapid advances in and mass popularity of visual technology certainly contributed to the way in which visuality has permeated the cultural imaginary of the twentieth (and twenty-first) century. By some accounts, there may have been a psychological or historical need for the ascension of the retinal sense and dominance of visual technology. If, as Marita Amm proposes, the nineteenth century was the ‘century of the eye,’ then the legacy only deepened in the twentieth century to include the eye’s homophonic relations.


I do not intend for this pun merely to invoke the rise of psychology; the additional connotation here is forming of the I via the eye. That is, in keeping with Walter Benjamin among others, with the advent of photography and the motion picture, the powerfully affective qualities of the ‘realistic’ visual image and the new ability to capture on film aspects of everyday experience unavailable to ordinary sense perception have given rise to a radically altered vision of the self.  This would be the linguistically constructed self, the bodiless self so immersed in its own seeing that its interpretative facility is monopolized by the retinal. So thorough is this monopoly that when confronted with non-visual sensory challenging works by artists such as Lygia Clark, Ernesto Neto, or Arakawa and Gins who are working, broadly, with phenomenological issues, or aural, sound-based installations like Christina Kubisch, Edwin Van Der Heide, or Ed Osborn’s sonic environments,  it understands them first in terms of their visual qualities.


Scrutiny of the dominance of the visual is not a contemporary phenomenon in art by any means. Duchamp’s Sixteen Miles of String (1942) is but a single example in the influential artist’s body of work that challenges the visual - consider also the visual tropes, such as the Ocular Witness, in the Large Glass. However, over the last thirty to forty years the seeming hegemony of the visual has come under the scalpel of critical discourse and practice that brings closer attention the sensorium and its individual senses. While it would be a gross oversimplification to suggest that sound art is an offshoot of such concerns, or even a response to them, sound art contributes to the debate directly and indirectly. Douglas Kahn’s phenomenal Noise Water Meat: A History of Sound in the Arts, for example, retells a significant portion of the modernist and post-modernist avant-garde aesthetic in terms of the significance of sound. The book is by no means just a revolutionized musicology; rather it foregrounds and uncovers the history of hearing and sound production that has been overshadowed by the rhetoric of the visual. Moreover, some of the work of another contributor to this issue of The Iowa Review Web, Paul DeMarinis, excavates old, mostly nineteenth century, sound technology and puts it to use as contemporary cultural and technological comment.


Indeed, technology is an important factor in much sound art and the history of sound, though its role is by no means easy to encapsulate, as I think all of the pieces in this issue demonstrate. Just as film and visual technology have been used to investigate imperceptible phenomena, so too has audio technology. The frequently retold story of John Cage entering the anechoic chamber at Harvard for the first time when he could hear the pulsing of his blood and the activity of his nervous system is but one example of the use of technology (in this case the paradoxical application of sound-proofing to enable hearing) to hear small, imperceptible sounds. Perhaps a more pertinent historical example would be Cage’s Cartridge Music, which required “microphones and cartridges...connected to amplifiers that go to loudspeakers [because] the majority of sounds produced [are] small and require amplification in order to be heard” (qtd. in Kahn, 194). Certainly, one aspect of Ed Osborn’s Harvester is the use of microphones to produce from movement and proximity sounds from ostensible silence. Such use of technology to ‘pick-up’ the normally inaudible, in fact, ties into a much longer history of sound related technology like Thomas Watson’s (Alexander Graham Bell’s assistant) use of a telephone circuit to pick up the crackle he claimed contained voices from another planet.


In Brandon La Belle’s piece in this issue, sound technology, a boom box in this case, is shown to have become both a community and identity-forming instrument. By focusing on the social and reception side of sound technology, La Belle also opens up discourse to the significance sound plays in our ordinary lives - and not always sound we want to hear - in contrast to the usual focus on the visual. The contribution of sound in our day-to-day lives is a point seldom put to critical use. Perhaps the best known, but not by any means well known, insight into this aspect of sound is in Benjamin’s Arcades Project in the frequent allusions to Dickens’s need to be amongst the sounds and noises of the city in order to write.


Working another angle on the social is Alexis Baghat’s piece on anti-authoritarian writing. This piece, part of his ‘Lecture’ series, uses a number of digital manipulations to call into question the written text. Unlike the arguments privileging speech over writing, Baghat takes on the notion of inscription more generally with such alterations and layering of his, and another unidentified woman’s, recorded speech as simultaneously a contributing factor to the presentation of the piece, an effacement of the recorded voice, and a means through which his speech is actually made. The other audio piece from ADACHI Tomomi is also an experiment into vocal effacement and meaning, though in this case, as the titles (in Japanese) humorously suggest, it is effacement that comes close to being something else - an octopus, for example.


The pieces in this issue of The Iowa Review Web are by no means a complete representation of the various trends in sound art. My hope was to at least represent a few threads and to hopefully bring to the attention of all of us who have a stake in New Media that sound is not just a decoration or a vehicle to add flavor to a piece (though it can be that too!). I am aware of the oddity of critiquing vision at the beginning of this piece and then to have two of the gallery artist’s works in video. On that point, I simply want to emphasize that to critique the dominance of the visual is not to want it done away with. Rather, what interests me is a non-hierarchy of the senses - something I believe all the gallery pieces achieve.


In closing, some words of thanks are due to a fair number of people who have helped to make this issue come together. First, I would like to thank all of the contributors to this issue who have been extremely patient as we faced delay after delay. As readers may have noticed, The Iowa Review Web has not published since last November. We have had numerous difficulties with the technical aspects of this issue and thanks for overcoming these are due to Jon Winet, Michael Peter, Craig Dietrich, and everyone else in Intermedia at The University of Iowa. Finally, while only ‘doing their jobs’, Thom Swiss, Merrie Snell, and Brittany Duff have all been instrumental in getting this issue together.
